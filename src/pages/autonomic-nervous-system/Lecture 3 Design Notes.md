## Phenylephrine, Pseudoephedrine, Albuterol, Salmeterol, Terbutaline, Midodrine

### Opening Frame
Begin by asking students a question that exposes a gap: if epinephrine is such a powerful and versatile adrenergic agonist, why do we need any other sympathomimetic drugs? Let them attempt an answer before providing one. The answer is selectivity and pharmacokinetics — clinicians frequently need to stimulate one receptor subtype without activating others, and they often need drugs that can be taken orally, last longer than a few minutes, or penetrate the CNS. Non-catecholamines were developed precisely to meet those needs.

Introduce the defining structural distinction: non-catecholamines lack the catechol nucleus (the benzene ring with two adjacent hydroxyl groups). This seemingly small chemical difference has large pharmacokinetic consequences — they are resistant to degradation by COMT, and most are resistant to MAO as well. The result is longer duration of action, oral bioavailability, and in some cases meaningful CNS penetration. Use this structural point not as chemistry for its own sake but as the explanation for everything that follows.

Frame this lecture as the "applied selectivity" lecture. Each drug here is a case study in what happens when receptor selectivity is engineered deliberately — the benefits in clinical precision, and the trade-offs in what is lost.

### Phenylephrine
Phenylephrine is a selective α1 agonist with negligible β activity. Its primary action is vasoconstriction — it raises blood pressure by increasing peripheral vascular resistance without directly stimulating the heart. Because it does not activate β1 receptors, it does not increase heart rate or contractility, and in fact commonly produces **reflex bradycardia** as the baroreceptors respond to rising blood pressure by increasing vagal tone. This reflex bradycardia is an important distinguishing feature and a frequent exam question.

Receptor profile: Selective α1, no meaningful β activity.

Clinical forms: Phenylephrine exists in multiple formulations that reflect its α1 selectivity — IV infusion for intraoperative hypotension, nasal spray and oral decongestant for nasal congestion (local vasoconstriction of nasal mucosa), and ophthalmic drops for pupillary dilation (mydriasis without cycloplegia, since it does not affect the ciliary muscle's muscarinic receptors).

Contrast with epinephrine: Both activate α1, but epinephrine also activates β receptors. For situations where pure vasopressor effect is needed without cardiac stimulation — such as spinal anesthesia-induced hypotension — phenylephrine is preferred precisely because it lacks β activity.

### Pseudoephedrine
Pseudoephedrine is a mixed-acting sympathomimetic — it acts both directly at adrenergic receptors and indirectly by promoting the release of norepinephrine from presynaptic terminals. Its primary clinical use is nasal decongestion via α1-mediated vasoconstriction of nasal mucosal blood vessels, reducing swelling and improving airflow.

Key pharmacokinetic feature: Pseudoephedrine is orally bioavailable and has meaningful CNS penetration, which accounts for its stimulant side effects (insomnia, anxiety, restlessness). This CNS activity is also why it has abuse potential — it is a precursor in the illicit synthesis of methamphetamine, which is why it is regulated under the Combat Methamphetamine Epidemic Act and kept behind the pharmacy counter with purchase quantity limits. Students entering practice need to know this regulation exists and why.

Cardiovascular effects: Pseudoephedrine can raise blood pressure and heart rate, making it a concern in patients with hypertension, heart disease, hyperthyroidism, or those taking MAO inhibitors. The interaction with MAO inhibitors is potentially life-threatening — combining a drug that releases NE with one that prevents NE breakdown can cause a hypertensive crisis. Flag this explicitly as a high-priority drug interaction.

Distinguish from phenylephrine: Both are used as oral decongestants, but pseudoephedrine is more effective because of its indirect NE-releasing mechanism. Phenylephrine as an oral decongestant has faced questions about efficacy, with some evidence suggesting it may not be meaningfully more effective than placebo at standard oral doses — another clinical nuance worth raising.

### Albuterol
Albuterol (salbutamol outside the US) is a selective β2 agonist and one of the most clinically important drugs in this entire course. Its receptor selectivity is the direct application of everything students have been building — β2 receptors in bronchial smooth muscle mediate bronchodilation, and albuterol's selectivity for β2 over β1 allows it to open the airways without causing the degree of cardiac stimulation that a non-selective agent would.

Receptor profile: Selective β2, minimal β1 at standard doses.

Onset and duration: Albuterol is a **short-acting β2 agonist (SABA)** — onset within minutes via inhalation, duration 4–6 hours. This makes it the prototype **rescue inhaler** for acute bronchospasm in asthma and COPD. Students must understand the difference between rescue (short-acting, symptom-driven) and maintenance (long-acting, scheduled) therapy — this distinction drives much of the patient education around inhaler use.

Adverse effects: Despite β2 selectivity, albuterol is not perfectly selective. At higher doses, β1 activation causes tachycardia and palpitations. β2 activation in the liver causes glycogenolysis, raising blood glucose — a consideration in diabetic patients. β2 activation in skeletal muscle can cause tremor, which is a common complaint at higher doses. Hypokalemia is a less intuitive but important adverse effect: β2 stimulation drives potassium into cells, lowering serum levels. This is clinically significant in patients with baseline electrolyte disturbances or cardiac disease.

Route of administration: Inhaled delivery is strongly preferred over systemic routes — it delivers drug directly to the bronchial smooth muscle, achieves therapeutic effect at much lower doses, and substantially reduces systemic adverse effects. Students should understand inhaler technique as a nursing education priority, since improper technique (not coordinating actuation with inhalation, not shaking the inhaler, not using a spacer when appropriate) significantly reduces drug delivery.

### Salmeterol
Salmeterol is a **long-acting β2 agonist (LABA)** — the structural modifications that make it non-catecholamine also make it highly lipophilic, allowing it to anchor in the cell membrane near the β2 receptor and produce effects lasting 12 hours. This pharmacokinetic property makes it fundamentally unsuitable as a rescue agent — its onset is too slow (15–20 minutes) — but ideal for scheduled maintenance therapy to prevent bronchospasm.

The critical safety point: salmeterol **must not be used as monotherapy** in asthma. Use without an inhaled corticosteroid is associated with increased risk of asthma-related death, likely because the bronchodilation masks worsening inflammation without treating it. It is always prescribed in fixed-dose combination with an inhaled corticosteroid (e.g., fluticasone/salmeterol — Advair). This is one of the most important patient education points for any patient with asthma receiving a LABA — they must understand it is a scheduled maintenance medication, not a rescue inhaler.

Contrast the SABA/LABA distinction clearly and explicitly: albuterol is for *acute relief*, salmeterol is for *scheduled prevention*. A patient reaching for salmeterol during an acute asthma attack is making a potentially dangerous error.

### Terbutaline
Terbutaline is a selective β2 agonist pharmacologically similar to albuterol but with a notably broader clinical application in nursing practice: **tocolysis** — the suppression of preterm uterine contractions. β2 receptors in uterine smooth muscle mediate relaxation, and terbutaline exploits this to delay preterm labor.

Tocolytic use: Terbutaline is used for short-term suppression of preterm contractions (typically to allow time for antenatal corticosteroids to be administered for fetal lung maturity). The FDA has issued a safety communication warning against long-term oral or injectable terbutaline for preterm labor due to serious maternal cardiovascular adverse effects and maternal deaths — emphasize this restriction explicitly. It is not approved for long-term tocolysis.

Adverse effects in the tocolytic context: Because terbutaline is systemic (subcutaneous or IV in this setting), β1 effects become clinically relevant. Maternal tachycardia is common and expected; the threshold for concern is typically a maternal heart rate above 120 bpm. Pulmonary edema is a rare but serious complication requiring prompt recognition. Fetal tachycardia can occur because the drug crosses the placenta. Hypokalemia and hyperglycemia (same mechanism as with albuterol) require monitoring, particularly in patients with gestational diabetes.

Nursing monitoring in tocolysis: Continuous maternal cardiac monitoring, hourly urine output, lung auscultation for pulmonary edema, and fetal heart rate monitoring are all indicated. Students rotating through labor and delivery will encounter this drug in practice, making this one of the highest clinical-relevance topics in the lecture.

### Midodrine
Midodrine is a selective α1 agonist used specifically for **orthostatic hypotension** — a drop in blood pressure upon standing that causes dizziness, presyncope, or syncope. It is the only oral α1 agonist in this group, and its selectivity makes it useful for restoring vascular tone without the cardiac stimulation of broader adrenergic agents.

Mechanism: Midodrine is a prodrug converted to its active form (desglymidodrine) after absorption. The active metabolite causes arterial and venous vasoconstriction via α1 receptors, raising blood pressure in the standing position.

Critical adverse effect — supine hypertension: Because midodrine raises blood pressure by vasoconstriction regardless of posture, it causes significant hypertension when the patient is lying flat. Patients must **not** take midodrine within 4 hours of bedtime and must sleep with the head of the bed elevated. This is a non-negotiable patient education point and a frequent source of medication errors. Nurses should reinforce this instruction with every administration.

Patient population: Midodrine is commonly used in patients with autonomic neuropathy (including diabetic neuropathy), Parkinson's disease, or those with orthostatic hypotension following prolonged bed rest or deconditioning. It is also used in select patients with refractory hypotension due to dialysis or spinal cord injury.

## Differences from Catecholamines — Oral Bioavailability, Duration, CNS Penetration

### Oral Bioavailability
The catecholamine structure is the target of two inactivating enzymes — MAO (monoamine oxidase) and COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase) — that are present in the gut wall, liver, and plasma. Catecholamines administered orally are destroyed before reaching systemic circulation. Non-catecholamines, by lacking the catechol hydroxyl groups, resist COMT degradation and are variably resistant to MAO. The result is that most can be taken orally and achieve clinically meaningful systemic levels.

This is not merely a pharmacokinetics footnote — it is the reason most of these drugs exist in their current clinical forms. Pseudoephedrine as an oral decongestant, albuterol as an oral tablet (though inhalation is preferred), terbutaline for subcutaneous and oral use, and midodrine as an oral α1 agonist are all possible only because the catechol structure was modified. The teaching point: structural chemistry drives clinical utility.

### Duration of Action
MAO and COMT degrade catecholamines within minutes, which is why catecholamine infusions must be continuous and are measured in micrograms per kilogram per minute. Non-catecholamines are metabolized more slowly, extending their duration of action from minutes to hours. This longer duration enables intermittent dosing, outpatient use, and inhaled formulations designed for scheduled maintenance therapy.

Salmeterol is the extreme example in this lecture: its lipophilic structure allows it to embed in cell membranes near the β2 receptor, producing effects lasting 12 hours from a single inhaled dose. This is an almost entirely pharmacokinetic achievement — the prolonged duration is a direct consequence of removing the catechol structure and engineering high lipophilicity.

Duration also introduces a double-edged consideration: longer duration means less frequent dosing (a benefit) but also longer duration of side effects if they occur (a liability). A patient who develops tachycardia from an albuterol nebulizer treatment will recover quickly as the drug is cleared; a patient who develops adverse effects from a long-acting agent may experience them for many hours.

### CNS Penetration
The catechol structure is relatively polar and crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly. Non-catecholamines vary considerably in their CNS penetration depending on their lipophilicity, and this variation has direct clinical consequences.

Pseudoephedrine has meaningful CNS penetration, producing stimulant effects (insomnia, anxiety, restlessness) and carrying abuse potential. This is why it is regulated, and why patients with anxiety disorders, insomnia, or psychiatric conditions should use it cautiously if at all.

Albuterol, salmeterol, and terbutaline — particularly when inhaled — have limited CNS penetration at therapeutic doses. The inhaled route further minimizes systemic exposure, reducing CNS effects to near zero under normal conditions. This is part of why inhalation is so strongly preferred for respiratory agents.

Midodrine has minimal CNS penetration, which is clinically desirable — its purpose is peripheral vasoconstriction, and CNS activity would introduce unnecessary risk.

This variation in CNS penetration is a useful framework for discussing why route of administration matters beyond simply "getting the drug to the target." Inhalation of albuterol is not just more efficient — it actively reduces CNS and cardiovascular exposure compared to systemic delivery.

## Clinical Uses — Nasal Congestion, Asthma/COPD, Preterm Labor, Orthostatic Hypotension

### Nasal Congestion
Both phenylephrine and pseudoephedrine are used as nasal decongestants via α1-mediated vasoconstriction of the nasal mucosal vasculature. Reducing blood flow to the nasal mucosa decreases swelling and opens the nasal passages. The clinical difference between the two comes down to route, efficacy, and risk profile.

Topical nasal phenylephrine (spray) works locally with minimal systemic absorption and is appropriate for short-term use. Oral pseudoephedrine produces more reliable systemic decongestant effects but carries cardiovascular risks and CNS stimulant effects. Both agents are subject to **rhinitis medicamentosa** (rebound congestion) with prolonged topical use — though this is more commonly associated with oxymetazoline (an imidazoline α agonist) than with phenylephrine nasal spray.

Patient education priorities: limit use to no more than 3–5 days for topical agents; avoid in hypertension; avoid pseudoephedrine with MAO inhibitors or in patients with hyperthyroidism, prostate enlargement (risk of urinary retention via α1 bladder neck constriction), or cardiovascular disease.

### Asthma and COPD
Albuterol and salmeterol represent the two poles of β2 agonist therapy in obstructive airway disease — rescue and maintenance — and together they anchor the pharmacological management of both asthma and COPD.

In **asthma**, the stepwise treatment approach (GINA guidelines) positions albuterol as the universal rescue agent at every step, with LABAs added as maintenance agents at moderate-to-severe disease levels, always in combination with inhaled corticosteroids. The absolute prohibition on LABA monotherapy in asthma must be reinforced — it is a regulatory restriction (FDA black box warning) as well as a clinical one.

In **COPD**, the pharmacological approach differs meaningfully from asthma. LABAs — along with long-acting muscarinic antagonists (LAMAs, covered in the cholinergic unit) — are the primary maintenance agents. Albuterol remains the rescue agent for acute exacerbations. The inflammatory mechanism in COPD is less steroid-responsive than in asthma, so the role of inhaled corticosteroids is more nuanced and generally reserved for patients with frequent exacerbations.

Connect to the upcoming cholinergic unit: ipratropium (a muscarinic antagonist bronchodilator) is frequently combined with albuterol in acute COPD exacerbations. Students who understand why both a β2 agonist and a muscarinic antagonist cause bronchodilation — through different mechanisms — will understand the rationale for combination therapy.

### Preterm Labor
Terbutaline's use in tocolysis is a focused, high-acuity application that nursing students will encounter in obstetric rotations. The clinical context is well-defined: preterm labor (contractions with cervical change before 37 weeks) that requires short-term delay, typically to allow 48 hours for antenatal corticosteroid administration (betamethasone or dexamethasone) to accelerate fetal lung maturity.

The mechanism is straightforward: β2 receptors in myometrial smooth muscle, when stimulated, activate adenylyl cyclase, raising intracellular cAMP, which reduces intracellular calcium and inhibits myosin light chain kinase — the net result is smooth muscle relaxation and cessation of contractions.

Frame the risk-benefit discussion explicitly: terbutaline has real maternal risks (tachycardia, pulmonary edema, hypokalemia, hyperglycemia) but the benefit of gaining 48 hours for corticosteroid administration is clinically significant for fetal outcomes. The decision to use it is a physician or midwife determination; the nurse's role is intensive monitoring, recognizing adverse effects early, and educating the patient about what to expect.

### Orthostatic Hypotension
Midodrine's indication is one of the more condition-specific applications in this lecture. Orthostatic hypotension is defined as a drop in systolic BP of ≥20 mmHg or diastolic BP of ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing. It is common in older adults, in patients with autonomic neuropathy (particularly diabetic), in patients on prolonged bed rest, and in those with Parkinson's disease or multiple system atrophy.

Non-pharmacological measures are always first-line: compression stockings, increased salt and fluid intake, slow positional changes, elevating the head of the bed at night. Midodrine is added when these measures are insufficient.

The nursing role with midodrine is largely educational. Patients must understand the supine hypertension risk and the bedtime restriction. They should be counseled to take the drug during waking hours only — typically three times daily, with the last dose at least 4 hours before lying down. Measuring blood pressure in both supine and standing positions during titration is essential to assess both therapeutic effect (improved standing BP) and adverse effect (supine hypertension).
