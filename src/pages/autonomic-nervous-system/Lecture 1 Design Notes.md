## Sympathetic vs. Parasympathetic Branches — Anatomy and Function

### The Big Picture Frame
Open by establishing *why* the ANS matters to pharmacology. The ANS is the primary target of a massive category of drugs students will administer throughout their careers — cardiac drugs, respiratory drugs, GI drugs, urologic drugs, ophthalmologic drugs, and emergency drugs all work by modifying ANS activity. Every drug in this course is either mimicking or blocking one of the two branches. Make that explicit before a single anatomical fact is presented.

### Structural Anatomy
Cover the two-neuron efferent pathway that both branches share: a preganglionic neuron originating in the CNS synapses in a ganglion, and a postganglionic neuron travels from the ganglion to the target organ. This structural commonality is important because it sets up why there are two distinct neurotransmitters and two distinct receptor families.

For the **sympathetic branch**, the preganglionic neurons originate in the thoracic and lumbar spinal cord (T1–L2), giving rise to the term *thoracolumbar outflow*. The ganglia are located close to the spinal cord in the paravertebral sympathetic chain, meaning preganglionic fibers are short and postganglionic fibers are long. The adrenal medulla is a special case — it is embryologically derived from sympathetic ganglionic tissue and acts as a modified postganglionic neuron, releasing epinephrine (and some norepinephrine) directly into the bloodstream.

For the **parasympathetic branch**, preganglionic neurons originate in the brainstem (cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X) and the sacral spinal cord (S2–S4) — hence *craniosacral outflow*. The ganglia are located very close to or within the target organ, meaning preganglionic fibers are long and postganglionic fibers are short.

### Functional Anatomy: The Fight-or-Flight vs. Rest-and-Digest Framework
Introduce the classic mnemonic framework, but immediately push past it into organ-by-organ specifics. Students must be able to predict what *each organ* does under sympathetic vs. parasympathetic dominance. Walk through each target organ systematically:

**Heart:** Sympathetic increases heart rate (chronotropy) and force of contraction (inotropy). Parasympathetic slows heart rate (the vagal brake) and reduces AV node conduction velocity.

**Blood vessels:** Sympathetic causes vasoconstriction of most vessels (especially skin, viscera, and skeletal muscle at rest) and vasodilation of skeletal muscle vessels during exercise. Parasympathetic has minimal influence on most vascular beds.

**Lungs:** Sympathetic causes bronchodilation and reduces secretions. Parasympathetic causes bronchoconstriction and increases mucus secretion.

**GI tract:** Sympathetic reduces motility and secretion, and constricts GI sphincters. Parasympathetic increases motility, secretion, and relaxes sphincters.

**Urinary bladder:** Sympathetic relaxes the detrusor muscle (storing urine) and contracts the internal urethral sphincter. Parasympathetic contracts the detrusor (voiding) and relaxes the sphincter.

**Eye:** Sympathetic dilates the pupil (mydriasis) via the radial muscle and causes far-focus by relaxing the ciliary muscle. Parasympathetic constricts the pupil (miosis) via the sphincter muscle and causes near-focus (accommodation) by contracting the ciliary muscle.

**Skin:** Sympathetic stimulates sweating (notably a cholinergic effect despite sympathetic innervation — a useful teaching exception) and causes piloerection.

**Liver and metabolic:** Sympathetic promotes glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis, increasing blood glucose. Parasympathetic promotes glycogen synthesis.

**Salivary glands:** Sympathetic produces thick, viscous saliva. Parasympathetic produces large volumes of watery saliva.

Emphasize that this organ-by-organ knowledge is the direct basis for predicting drug side effects. A drug that blocks the parasympathetic branch will cause dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, tachycardia, and mydriasis — because those are exactly what happens when parasympathetic tone is removed.

## Neurotransmitters — Norepinephrine, Epinephrine, Acetylcholine

### Synthesis, Storage, and Release
Students need more than "NE is the sympathetic neurotransmitter." They need to understand how these molecules are made, stored, released, and terminated, because drug mechanisms frequently intervene at each of those steps.

**Norepinephrine (NE):** Synthesized in the postganglionic sympathetic neuron from tyrosine via a multi-step pathway (tyrosine → DOPA → dopamine → NE). Stored in vesicles at the axon terminal. Released by exocytosis in response to an action potential. After release, it acts on postsynaptic receptors and is then primarily removed by reuptake (Uptake-1) back into the presynaptic terminal, with a smaller portion degraded by MAO and COMT. This reuptake mechanism is the target of several drug classes students will encounter later.

**Epinephrine (EPI):** Synthesized in the adrenal medulla from NE (via PNMT enzyme). Released directly into the bloodstream as a hormone, meaning its effects are systemic and longer-lasting than synaptically released NE. EPI has higher affinity for beta receptors than NE does — this difference in receptor affinity explains why EPI and NE produce somewhat different cardiovascular profiles at comparable doses.

**Acetylcholine (ACh):** Synthesized in both sympathetic and parasympathetic preganglionic neurons, all parasympathetic postganglionic neurons, and at the neuromuscular junction from choline and acetyl-CoA via choline acetyltransferase. Unlike NE, ACh is terminated primarily by enzymatic degradation — acetylcholinesterase (AChE) breaks it down almost instantly in the synapse into choline and acetate. This rapid degradation is why ACh itself is not used therapeutically, and why anticholinesterase drugs have such profound and rapid effects.

### The Dual Neurotransmitter Rule
Introduce the rule that ACh is the neurotransmitter at *all* autonomic ganglia (both sympathetic and parasympathetic) and at the neuromuscular junction, while NE is the neurotransmitter at most sympathetic postganglionic synapses and ACh at parasympathetic postganglionic synapses. A clear diagram of the full pathway — from spinal cord to target organ, labeling each synapse and its neurotransmitter — is worth spending real time on.

The key insight students most often miss: when you block a ganglionic receptor (nicotinic), you block *both* branches simultaneously. This is why ganglionic blockers are largely obsolete — their side effect profile is unmanageable — and it sets up why selective receptor targeting is so clinically important.

## Receptor Subtypes

### Adrenergic Receptors

**Alpha-1 (α1):** Located postsynaptically on vascular smooth muscle, the eye (radial muscle), and the internal urethral sphincter. Stimulation causes vasoconstriction, pupil dilation, and urinary sphincter contraction. This is the receptor responsible for maintaining blood pressure via vascular tone. Drug implication: α1 blockers (prazosin) lower blood pressure and relieve urinary obstruction in BPH.

**Alpha-2 (α2):** Located presynaptically on sympathetic nerve terminals (autoreceptors) and in the CNS. Stimulation of presynaptic α2 acts as a negative feedback mechanism — it reduces further NE release. CNS α2 stimulation reduces sympathetic outflow from the brain, lowering blood pressure. Drug implication: clonidine acts here. This receptor also explains why some drugs that appear to reduce sympathetic activity are actually sympathomimetics acting presynaptically.

**Beta-1 (β1):** Located predominantly in the heart and kidney. Cardiac β1 stimulation increases heart rate, contractility, and AV conduction velocity. Renal β1 stimulation releases renin, increasing angiotensin II and aldosterone. Drug implications: β1 agonists (dobutamine) support failing hearts; β1-selective blockers (metoprolol) treat hypertension and heart failure without bronchospasm.

**Beta-2 (β2):** Located in bronchial smooth muscle, uterine smooth muscle, vascular smooth muscle of skeletal muscle, and the liver. Stimulation causes bronchodilation, uterine relaxation, skeletal muscle vasodilation, and glycogenolysis. Drug implications: β2 agonists (albuterol) treat asthma; terbutaline delays preterm labor. The reason non-selective beta blockers are contraindicated in asthma is β2 blockade causing bronchoconstriction.

**Beta-3 (β3):** Briefly note this receptor in the bladder detrusor muscle. Stimulation causes detrusor relaxation — the target of mirabegron, a newer agent for overactive bladder. Not a primary focus of this course but worth flagging as an emerging class.

For adrenergic receptors, the key teaching tool is a selectivity spectrum: full agonist activity across all receptors (isoproterenol) at one end, through mixed agents (epinephrine, NE), to highly selective agents (phenylephrine for α1, dobutamine for β1, albuterol for β2). Students who understand this spectrum can reason about any adrenergic drug.

### Cholinergic Receptors

**Muscarinic receptors (M1–M5):** Located on all parasympathetic target organs (heart, lungs, GI tract, bladder, eye, glands) and on sweat glands (sympathetic but cholinergic). For nursing pharmacology, the distinction between M1–M5 subtypes is less critical than understanding the general effects of muscarinic stimulation vs. blockade. However, M2 (cardiac) and M3 (smooth muscle, glands) are worth naming, as drug selectivity for these subtypes underlies agents like ipratropium and newer antimuscarinics. Introduce the SLUDGE mnemonic here as a preview — Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, GI distress, Emesis — and flag it for full treatment in the cholinergic unit.

**Nicotinic receptors (NM and NN):** Two subtypes serve completely different functions. Nicotinic-neuronal (NN) receptors are located at all autonomic ganglia and the adrenal medulla — they mediate transmission between pre- and postganglionic neurons in both branches. Nicotinic-muscle (NM) receptors are located at the neuromuscular junction and mediate skeletal muscle contraction. This distinction is clinically crucial: drugs acting at NM receptors (succinylcholine, rocuronium) cause muscle paralysis; drugs acting at NN receptors affect the entire autonomic system. Misunderstanding this distinction leads to real clinical errors in reasoning about neuromuscular blockers.

## Agonist vs. Antagonist Pharmacology — A Framework for the Course

This section is less about new content and more about giving students the **cognitive framework** they will carry through the entire course. It deserves deliberate, unhurried teaching.

### Define the Terms Precisely
An **agonist** binds to a receptor and activates it, producing a biological response. An **antagonist** binds to the receptor and blocks it, preventing the endogenous neurotransmitter (or an agonist drug) from activating it. A **partial agonist** activates the receptor but produces a submaximal response even at full receptor occupancy. These definitions should be crisp and exact — vague language here leads to conceptual errors later.

### The Derivation Game
Introduce the four-step reasoning process students will use for every drug in the course:
1. Identify the receptor the drug acts on.
2. Determine whether it is an agonist or antagonist.
3. Recall what activation of that receptor normally produces.
4. If agonist: the drug produces those effects. If antagonist: the drug produces the *opposite* of those effects.

Walk through two complete worked examples. First: "Albuterol is a β2 agonist. β2 receptors in the lungs mediate bronchodilation. Therefore albuterol causes bronchodilation — this is why it treats asthma." Second: "Atropine is a muscarinic antagonist. Muscarinic receptors in the heart mediate slowing of heart rate. Blocking those receptors removes the parasympathetic brake on heart rate. Therefore atropine causes tachycardia — this is why it treats bradycardia." Students who can follow both of those chains of logic are ready for the rest of the course.

### Selectivity and Why It Matters Clinically
Introduce selectivity as a spectrum, not a binary. No drug is perfectly selective, and at high doses, even selective drugs begin to hit off-target receptors. This is why metoprolol, a β1-selective blocker, is relatively safe in asthma at low doses but can still cause bronchoconstriction at high doses. Teaching selectivity as a concept — rather than a fact about individual drugs — gives students a framework for understanding dose-dependent side effects across the entire course.

### Therapeutic vs. Adverse Effects Are the Same Mechanism
For autonomic drugs, adverse effects are usually not random toxicities — they are the *predictable consequences of receptor activation in tissues where you don't want the effect.* Albuterol causes tachycardia because it has some β1 activity in addition to its intended β2 activity. Oxybutynin causes dry mouth, constipation, and urinary retention because blocking muscarinic receptors affects every organ with muscarinic innervation, not just the bladder. Students who understand this can predict adverse effects from mechanism rather than memorizing them — and that is the difference between a student who understands pharmacology and one who is just trying to survive the exam.

### Preview the Course Arc
Close the week by showing students a visual map of the entire course organized around this framework: sympathetic branch (agonists, then antagonists), parasympathetic branch (agonists, then antagonists), then clinical integration. Make clear that every drug they will encounter for the next 15 weeks is a variation on this theme. This "you are here" map reduces cognitive overload as the course progresses and gives students a sense of the architecture they are building — which makes each new topic feel like adding a room to a house rather than starting over from scratch.
