## Epinephrine, Norepinephrine, Dopamine, Dobutamine, Isoproterenol

### Opening Frame
Begin by connecting to the receptor map students built in Lecture 1. This lecture is where that map becomes clinically alive. Every drug covered today is a direct agonist at adrenergic receptors — it either mimics the action of the sympathetic nervous system or amplifies it. Students who have internalized the receptor effects from Week 1 should be able to predict the majority of what follows before you tell them. Use that as a teaching strategy: present the receptor profile of each drug first, ask students what they expect to happen clinically, then confirm or refine.

Introduce the term **catecholamine** explicitly. A catecholamine is a compound with a catechol nucleus (benzene ring with two adjacent hydroxyl groups) and an amine side chain. The clinically relevant catecholamines are epinephrine, norepinephrine, dopamine, and dobutamine. Isoproterenol is a synthetic catecholamine. What they share structurally determines what they share pharmacokinetically: all catecholamines are rapidly inactivated by MAO and COMT, they cannot be given orally (destroyed in the GI tract and liver on first pass), and they have short durations of action. This is why they are almost exclusively administered by injection or IV infusion.

### Epinephrine
Epinephrine is the prototype catecholamine and the most pharmacologically broad — it is a full agonist at α1, α2, β1, and β2 receptors. At low doses, β effects dominate (vasodilation in skeletal muscle, bronchodilation, increased heart rate and contractility). At high doses, α effects dominate (vasoconstriction, marked increase in blood pressure). This dose-dependent receptor dominance is a critical concept students should understand and be able to explain.

Receptor profile: α1 (vasoconstriction, increased BP), α2 (presynaptic feedback, some vasoconstriction), β1 (increased HR, contractility, AV conduction), β2 (bronchodilation, uterine relaxation, glycogenolysis, skeletal muscle vasodilation).

### Norepinephrine
Norepinephrine is primarily an α1 and β1 agonist with minimal β2 activity. It causes potent vasoconstriction (α1) and increases cardiac contractility (β1), but because it raises peripheral vascular resistance so dramatically, reflex bradycardia often results despite its direct cardiac stimulation. This baroreceptor-mediated reflex bradycardia is a classic teaching point — the body's attempt to counter the NE-driven rise in blood pressure by slowing the heart. Compare this directly to epinephrine, where β2-mediated vasodilation partially offsets the α1 vasoconstriction.

Receptor profile: Strong α1, moderate β1, negligible β2.

### Dopamine
Dopamine is uniquely dose-dependent across three receptor systems, making it one of the most nuanced drugs in this category. Teach the dose-response relationship explicitly as three ranges:

**Low dose (1–5 mcg/kg/min):** Primarily activates dopamine receptors (D1) in renal and mesenteric vasculature, causing vasodilation and increased renal perfusion. Historically used to provide "renal protection," though evidence for this indication has weakened considerably — flag this as a clinical controversy.

**Moderate dose (5–10 mcg/kg/min):** β1 receptor effects predominate, increasing heart rate and contractility. Useful in cardiogenic shock where cardiac output needs support.

**High dose (>10 mcg/kg/min):** α1 effects dominate, causing significant vasoconstriction. Useful in distributive shock (sepsis) where vascular tone is lost, but at the cost of reduced perfusion to peripheral tissues.

Emphasize that dopamine is a precursor to norepinephrine in the synthetic pathway — its endogenous role is both as a neurotransmitter in its own right (CNS) and as a biosynthetic intermediate.

### Dobutamine
Dobutamine is primarily a β1-selective agonist with some β2 and minimal α activity. Its defining clinical characteristic is that it increases cardiac contractility (positive inotrope) without the degree of vasoconstriction or heart rate increase seen with other catecholamines. This makes it the preferred agent when the therapeutic goal is to improve cardiac output in heart failure without dramatically raising heart rate or blood pressure.

Contrast explicitly with dopamine: both are used in shock states, but dobutamine is preferred when the primary problem is pump failure (cardiogenic shock with adequate blood pressure) whereas dopamine or norepinephrine are preferred when the problem is vascular tone (septic/distributive shock with hypotension).

Receptor profile: Strong β1, mild β2, minimal α1.

### Isoproterenol
Isoproterenol is a non-selective β agonist (β1 and β2) with no α activity whatsoever. It causes marked increases in heart rate and contractility (β1) and pronounced bronchodilation and vasodilation (β2). Because of its potent, non-selective β stimulation and significant arrhythmia risk, it has been largely replaced in clinical practice by more selective agents. It is still occasionally used for temporary management of refractory bradycardia or AV block when other options have failed.

Its primary value in this course is as a teaching reference point: the pure β agonist against which selective agents are compared. Students who understand isoproterenol's effects can immediately understand why selective β1 agents (dobutamine) or selective β2 agents (albuterol) were developed.

## Mechanism of Action and Receptor Selectivity

### Direct Receptor Activation
All catecholamines in this lecture act by **direct receptor agonism** — they bind to adrenergic receptors and activate them, mimicking or amplifying the effects of endogenous sympathetic stimulation. This distinguishes them from indirect-acting sympathomimetics (covered in Lecture 3), which work by increasing NE release or blocking reuptake rather than activating receptors directly.

### Receptor Selectivity as a Clinical Tool
Use this lecture to consolidate the concept of receptor selectivity introduced in Lecture 1. Present the five catecholamines on a receptor selectivity grid — rows for each receptor (α1, α2, β1, β2), columns for each drug — and have students fill it in collaboratively. The completed grid makes the clinical differences between drugs immediately visible and becomes a reference tool for the rest of the unit.

Key selectivity distinctions to emphasize:

**Epinephrine vs. Norepinephrine:** Both activate α1 and β1, but EPI has significant β2 activity and NE does not. This explains why EPI causes bronchodilation and is the drug of choice in anaphylaxis (where both bronchoconstriction and hypotension must be reversed), while NE does not bronchodilate and is used primarily for vasopressor support.

**Dobutamine vs. Dopamine:** Similar β1 effects but dobutamine is far more receptor-selective and predictable. Dopamine's multi-receptor, dose-dependent profile makes it versatile but less controllable — a nursing consideration with real implications for titration.

**Isoproterenol vs. all others:** Pure β agonism with no α activity — the theoretical "ceiling" of beta stimulation against which all others are compared.

### The Concept of Intrinsic Efficacy
Briefly introduce intrinsic efficacy — the ability of a drug to activate a receptor once bound. All catecholamines covered today are full agonists, meaning they produce a maximal response at full receptor occupancy. This will become more relevant when partial agonists are introduced later in the course, but planting the concept now builds a more complete pharmacological framework.

## Clinical Uses — Anaphylaxis, Septic Shock, Cardiac Arrest, Heart Failure

### Anaphylaxis
Epinephrine is the first-line and only appropriate initial treatment for anaphylaxis. No other drug should be administered before it. Students must understand *why* epinephrine is the correct drug — not as a memorized fact, but as a logical consequence of its receptor profile:

- **α1 activation** reverses the massive vasodilation and hypotension driving anaphylactic shock by causing vasoconstriction and restoring blood pressure.
- **β2 activation** reverses bronchoconstriction, improving airflow.
- **β1 activation** supports cardiac output in the setting of distributive shock.

No other single drug addresses all three of these simultaneously. Antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts — they have a role, but they are not rescue medications and they act too slowly to treat the acute life-threatening phase.

Route and dose: IM injection into the anterolateral thigh (vastus lateralis) is preferred over subcutaneous injection because IM absorption is faster and more reliable, especially in shock states when peripheral perfusion is reduced. The auto-injector (EpiPen) delivers a standard 0.3 mg dose IM. IV epinephrine is reserved for cardiac arrest or refractory anaphylaxis under monitored conditions.

### Septic Shock
Septic shock involves massive vasodilation from inflammatory mediator release, leading to distributive shock — cardiac output may be preserved or even elevated, but peripheral vascular resistance collapses and perfusion pressure falls. Vasopressors are required when fluid resuscitation is insufficient to restore mean arterial pressure.

**Norepinephrine** is the first-line vasopressor for septic shock (per Surviving Sepsis guidelines). Its strong α1 activity restores vascular tone, and its moderate β1 activity supports cardiac output without the excessive tachycardia seen with epinephrine. Dopamine is an alternative but carries higher arrhythmia risk and is generally considered second-line.

**Vasopressin** (a non-catecholamine vasopressor) is frequently added as a catecholamine-sparing agent in refractory septic shock — worth flagging here to broaden students' clinical picture, even though it is outside the scope of this lecture.

### Cardiac Arrest
Epinephrine is the primary pharmacological agent in cardiac arrest management per ACLS protocols. Its role is to maintain coronary and cerebral perfusion pressure during CPR by causing peripheral vasoconstriction (α1), redirecting blood flow to vital organs. Its β1 effects also increase myocardial excitability, which may facilitate return of spontaneous circulation.

Important nuance: the evidence base for epinephrine in cardiac arrest is more complicated than students often assume. It reliably increases rates of return of spontaneous circulation but has not consistently demonstrated improved neurological outcomes. Raise this briefly — it models the kind of critical, evidence-based thinking nursing practice requires, and it prevents students from treating "epinephrine in arrest" as an unexamined dogma.

**Dopamine** is used in post-resuscitation care for hemodynamic support when hypotension persists after return of spontaneous circulation. Connect this back to dopamine's dose-dependent profile — moderate doses for cardiac support, higher doses if vasopressor effect is needed.

### Heart Failure
**Dobutamine** is the primary catecholamine used in acute decompensated heart failure when the goal is to increase cardiac output in a patient with severely reduced ejection fraction. By selectively activating β1 receptors, it increases contractility and cardiac output without the excessive vasoconstriction that would increase afterload (which is already elevated in failing hearts) or the excessive tachycardia that increases myocardial oxygen demand.

Dobutamine is used as a short-term bridge — during acute decompensation, while awaiting response to other therapies, or as palliation in end-stage heart failure. Long-term use increases mortality (a recurring theme with inotropes) and this is worth discussing with students as an example of the difference between short-term hemodynamic benefit and long-term outcomes.

**Dopamine** at moderate doses is an alternative inotrope in heart failure, particularly when hypotension is also present. Compare the two drugs directly: if the patient is hypotensive, dopamine's broader receptor activity may be preferred; if blood pressure is adequate and the goal is purely inotropic support, dobutamine is more selective and predictable.

## Nursing Considerations — IV Administration, Hemodynamic Monitoring, Extravasation Precautions

### IV Administration
All catecholamines in this lecture are administered parenterally — primarily by continuous IV infusion via infusion pump in monitored settings. Oral administration is not possible due to first-pass metabolism. Several nursing administration points deserve emphasis:

**Concentration and titration:** Catecholamines are weight-based (mcg/kg/min) and must be carefully calculated for each patient. Infusion rates are titrated to clinical response — most commonly to a target mean arterial pressure (MAP), heart rate, or urine output. Students must be comfortable with the concept of titration to effect rather than fixed dosing, as this requires continuous reassessment.

**Central venous access:** Norepinephrine, dopamine at high doses, and epinephrine infusions should ideally be administered through a central venous catheter. Peripheral administration increases extravasation risk (see below) and is generally reserved for emergency situations when central access is not yet available.

**Compatibility:** Catecholamines are typically mixed in normal saline or D5W. They are incompatible with alkaline solutions (sodium bicarbonate inactivates them) — a clinically relevant interaction that should be flagged explicitly. Never administer through the same IV line as sodium bicarbonate.

**Light sensitivity:** Dopamine and epinephrine solutions should be protected from light and discarded if discoloration occurs. Discoloration (pink or brown) indicates oxidative degradation.

### Hemodynamic Monitoring
Patients receiving catecholamine infusions require intensive hemodynamic monitoring. Students need to understand not just *what* to monitor but *why* each parameter matters in the context of the drug's mechanism:

**Heart rate:** All catecholamines with β1 activity can cause tachycardia. A heart rate consistently above 120–130 bpm is a concern for increased myocardial oxygen demand and potential arrhythmias. Dobutamine commonly causes a modest increase in heart rate; epinephrine can cause significant tachycardia at higher doses.

**Blood pressure:** Continuous arterial line monitoring is preferred for patients on vasopressors. Target MAP is typically ≥65 mmHg in shock states. Students should understand that both hypotension (undertreated) and hypertension (overtreated) have serious consequences and that titration is an active, ongoing nursing responsibility.

**Cardiac rhythm:** Continuous cardiac monitoring is mandatory. Catecholamines — particularly dopamine and epinephrine — can precipitate ventricular arrhythmias, especially in patients with ischemic heart disease or electrolyte imbalances. Any new arrhythmia in a patient on a catecholamine infusion must be assessed promptly and reported.

**Urine output:** An hourly urine output of ≥0.5 mL/kg/hr is a marker of adequate renal perfusion. Declining urine output despite vasopressor therapy suggests either inadequate resuscitation or worsening end-organ dysfunction. It is one of the most practical and immediate indicators of whether hemodynamic goals are being met.

**Peripheral perfusion:** Assess skin color, temperature, and capillary refill regularly. Progressive mottling, cyanosis, or cold extremities in a patient on high-dose vasopressors signals excessive vasoconstriction compromising peripheral perfusion — a sign that dosing may need to be reassessed.

### Extravasation Precautions
Catecholamines — particularly norepinephrine and dopamine — cause intense local vasoconstriction if they leak outside the vein. Extravasation can result in severe tissue ischemia, necrosis, and potentially limb loss. This is not a minor complication and students should treat it with appropriate gravity.

**Prevention:** Use the largest available peripheral vein if central access is unavailable. Inspect the IV site frequently — at least every 1–2 hours — for signs of infiltration: swelling, blanching, coolness, or pain at the insertion site.

**Recognition:** Blanching or pallor around the IV site is the earliest and most reliable sign of extravasation with vasopressors. Do not wait for overt tissue breakdown to act.

**Treatment:** Stop the infusion immediately. The antidote for catecholamine extravasation is **phentolamine** — an alpha-adrenergic blocker that reverses the local vasoconstriction. It is injected subcutaneously around the affected area using a fine-gauge needle. Time is critical: phentolamine is most effective when administered within 12 hours of extravasation. This is an emergency intervention and students must know to notify the provider immediately.

Phentolamine as the antidote for extravasation is a high-yield nursing pharmacology fact. It is frequently tested on NCLEX and represents exactly the kind of mechanistic reasoning this course aims to develop — the antidote works because it blocks the same α1 receptors that are causing the vasoconstriction.
