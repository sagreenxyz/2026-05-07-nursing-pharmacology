## Non-Selective Alpha Blockers — Phentolamine, Phenoxybenzamine

### Opening Frame
Begin by anchoring this lecture in the receptor map. Students have spent three lectures learning what adrenergic agonists do — now the logic inverts. Alpha blockers work by occupying α receptors and preventing norepinephrine or epinephrine from activating them. If students truly understand α1 activation (vasoconstriction, urinary sphincter contraction, pupil dilation), they can derive the effects of α1 blockade without being told: vasodilation, urinary sphincter relaxation, miosis. Spend the first few minutes of class running that derivation exercise explicitly — it reinforces the receptor-based reasoning framework and gives students immediate confidence that they can handle this material.

Establish the organizing principle for the lecture: non-selective alpha blockers block both α1 and α2 receptors, while selective α1 blockers target only α1. This distinction matters clinically because α2 blockade has its own consequences — particularly a reflex tachycardia mechanism that makes non-selective agents less desirable for long-term use. Build toward that point as the lecture progresses.

### Phentolamine
Phentolamine is a competitive, reversible, non-selective alpha blocker — it blocks both α1 and α2 receptors with roughly equal affinity. Because it is competitive, its effects can be overcome by sufficiently high concentrations of norepinephrine or epinephrine, which is relevant in the pheochromocytoma context where catecholamine surges can be extreme.

**Mechanism of reflex tachycardia:** This is the most important pharmacological concept unique to non-selective alpha blockers and deserves careful teaching. When α1 is blocked, vasodilation occurs and blood pressure falls. The baroreceptors detect this fall and trigger a compensatory increase in sympathetic outflow — including release of norepinephrine at the heart. Normally, presynaptic α2 receptors on the sympathetic nerve terminal would detect the rising NE and provide negative feedback, reducing further release. But phentolamine also blocks α2. Without that brake, the compensatory sympathetic surge is unopposed — norepinephrine floods the synapse, activates β1 receptors, and causes significant reflex tachycardia. This is the direct pharmacological consequence of α2 blockade and it explains why non-selective alpha blockers are generally poor choices for chronic hypertension management.

**Clinical uses of phentolamine:**

*Pheochromocytoma:* Phentolamine is used acutely to control hypertensive crises caused by catecholamine release from a pheochromocytoma. Because the tumor releases massive quantities of epinephrine and norepinephrine unpredictably, rapid-acting parenteral alpha blockade is needed. Phentolamine given IV can quickly reverse the vasoconstriction driving the hypertensive surge. Pre-operatively, patients with pheochromocytoma are managed with oral phenoxybenzamine (see below) to establish stable alpha blockade before surgery.

*Catecholamine extravasation antidote:* Students encountered this in Lecture 2. Phentolamine injected subcutaneously around an extravasation site of norepinephrine or dopamine reverses the local α1-mediated vasoconstriction, restoring perfusion and preventing tissue necrosis. Reinforce the mechanism: phentolamine blocks the same α1 receptors being activated by the extravasated catecholamine.

*Erectile dysfunction (historical):* Phentolamine has been used intracavernosally in combination with other agents for erectile dysfunction, exploiting α1 blockade-mediated vasodilation of penile vasculature. This use is largely supplanted by PDE5 inhibitors but worth brief mention.

**Route and duration:** Phentolamine is available for IV and IM injection. Its duration of action is short (15–30 minutes IV), which is clinically useful in acute settings where rapid titration is needed but limits its utility for chronic management.

### Phenoxybenzamine
Phenoxybenzamine is a non-competitive, irreversible, non-selective alpha blocker. Unlike phentolamine, it forms a permanent covalent bond with the α receptor, meaning its effects cannot be overcome by increased catecholamine concentrations — no matter how much norepinephrine or epinephrine is released, the blocked receptors remain blocked. New receptor protein must be synthesized before normal function returns, making the duration of action extremely long (days rather than hours).

This irreversibility is both phenoxybenzamine's greatest clinical asset and its greatest liability. In the context of pheochromocytoma surgery — where manipulation of the tumor can cause explosive, unpredictable catecholamine release — irreversible blockade is specifically desirable. The blocked receptors cannot be overcome by the surge, providing stable hemodynamic control throughout the procedure. This is why phenoxybenzamine is the standard agent for preoperative management of pheochromocytoma: it is started 1–2 weeks before surgery and titrated to establish full, irreversible alpha blockade.

The liability of irreversibility is that adverse effects cannot be rapidly reversed if they occur. Orthostatic hypotension, nasal congestion, and reflex tachycardia (via the same α2 mechanism as phentolamine) persist until new receptors are synthesized. Patients must be counseled extensively and monitored closely during titration.

**The epinephrine reversal phenomenon:** Introduce this classic pharmacological teaching point here. Normally, epinephrine raises blood pressure via α1 (vasoconstriction) and β2 (some vasodilation) and β1 (cardiac stimulation). When α receptors are fully blocked by phenoxybenzamine, the vasoconstrictive α1 effect of epinephrine is eliminated, but its vasodilatory β2 effect is unmasked. The net result is that epinephrine paradoxically *lowers* blood pressure in the setting of full alpha blockade. This is known as epinephrine reversal. It is a conceptual cornerstone of adrenergic pharmacology and a frequent high-level exam question — students who understand it demonstrate genuine mastery of the receptor framework.

## Selective α1 Blockers — Prazosin, Terazosin, Doxazosin, Tamsulosin, Alfuzosin

### The Selective α1 Advantage
Selective α1 blockers represent a significant clinical advance over non-selective agents for most indications. By sparing α2 receptors, they preserve the presynaptic negative feedback mechanism on norepinephrine release. The result is vasodilation and blood pressure reduction without the exaggerated reflex tachycardia that limits non-selective agents. This makes them far more suitable for chronic use in hypertension and BPH.

The family of selective α1 blockers shares a core pharmacological profile — α1 blockade → vasodilation → reduced peripheral vascular resistance → lower blood pressure, and α1 blockade at the bladder neck and prostate → reduced urethral resistance → improved urine flow. What differentiates the individual agents is primarily receptor subtype selectivity within the α1 family, pharmacokinetics (half-life, dosing frequency), and consequently their clinical niche.

### Prazosin
Prazosin is the prototype selective α1 blocker and the reference point for the entire class. It blocks α1 receptors in vascular smooth muscle, causing arteriolar and venous dilation, reducing preload and afterload, and lowering blood pressure. Because it spares α2, reflex tachycardia is minimal compared to non-selective agents.

Half-life is relatively short (2–4 hours), requiring two to three times daily dosing — a compliance disadvantage compared to longer-acting agents. For this reason, prazosin has been largely replaced by terazosin and doxazosin for hypertension management.

**Off-label use — PTSD nightmares:** Prazosin has a well-established off-label use for reducing nightmare frequency and severity in post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). The mechanism is CNS α1 blockade, which reduces noradrenergic activity during sleep. This application is widely used in veteran populations and is worth mentioning as an example of how receptor-based reasoning extends into unexpected clinical territory.

### Terazosin and Doxazosin
Terazosin and doxazosin are longer-acting selective α1 blockers that share the same fundamental mechanism as prazosin but offer once-daily dosing, improving adherence. Both are used for hypertension and BPH. Doxazosin has the longest half-life (approximately 22 hours) of the two, making it the most forgiving of missed doses.

Both agents are non-selective within the α1 receptor subtype family — they block α1A, α1B, and α1D subtypes roughly equally. This broad α1 blockade is pharmacologically appropriate for hypertension (where systemic vascular tone reduction is the goal) but introduces more systemic effects — including a more pronounced orthostatic hypotension risk — than the uroselective agents tamsulosin and alfuzosin.

Clinical context for hypertension: Alpha blockers as a drug class are no longer considered first-line antihypertensive agents following the ALLHAT trial, which demonstrated that doxazosin was inferior to chlorthalidone (a thiazide diuretic) for cardiovascular outcomes, including a significantly higher rate of heart failure. This finding dramatically changed prescribing practice. Alpha blockers are now typically used as add-on agents in resistant hypertension, or in patients where a secondary indication (such as BPH) makes them particularly attractive. Students should know this clinical history — it models how evidence changes practice.

### Tamsulosin
Tamsulosin is a selective α1A/α1D blocker with preferential affinity for the α1A subtype, which predominates in the smooth muscle of the prostate, bladder neck, and urethra. This **uroselective** profile means tamsulosin produces its primary therapeutic effect — relaxation of the bladder neck and prostate smooth muscle, improving urinary outflow in BPH — at doses that cause relatively little systemic vasodilation and therefore less orthostatic hypotension than prazosin, terazosin, or doxazosin.

For BPH, tamsulosin is often the preferred first-line pharmacological agent because of this improved tolerability and its minimal cardiovascular effects. Patients with BPH are frequently elderly and may already be on antihypertensive agents, making the orthostatic hypotension risk of non-uroselective agents clinically significant.

**Drug interaction — PDE5 inhibitors:** The combination of tamsulosin with sildenafil, tadalafil, or other PDE5 inhibitors used for erectile dysfunction significantly increases the risk of severe hypotension. Both drug classes cause vasodilation through different mechanisms, and their combination is additive. This interaction is clinically important in the BPH population, where erectile dysfunction is common and co-prescription is not unusual. Students should be aware of this interaction and the need for patient education.

**Intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (IFIS):** Tamsulosin is associated with a surgical complication called intraoperative floppy iris syndrome during cataract surgery. α1 receptors in the iris dilator muscle are responsible for maintaining pupil dilation; tamsulosin blocks these receptors, causing the iris to become flaccid during surgery, which complicates the procedure and increases complication risk. Patients scheduled for cataract surgery should notify their ophthalmologist if they are taking tamsulosin, and ideally discontinue it before surgery with their prescriber's guidance. This is a practical patient education point that is frequently missed.

### Alfuzosin
Alfuzosin is a uroselective α1 blocker with a similar clinical profile to tamsulosin — used for BPH, with relatively low rates of orthostatic hypotension compared to non-uroselective agents. It is not considered more selective for α1A than for other α1 subtypes at the receptor level, but its tissue distribution appears to favor the lower urinary tract in practice, producing a uroselective clinical profile.

One differentiating feature: alfuzosin carries a QT prolongation warning and should be used with caution in patients on other QT-prolonging medications or with baseline QT abnormalities. This is worth noting as an example of how adverse effects can extend beyond the expected receptor-mediated profile — not all drug toxicity is predictable from mechanism alone.

## Clinical Uses — Hypertension, BPH, Pheochromocytoma

### Hypertension
Alpha blockers lower blood pressure primarily through α1-mediated arteriolar and venous dilation, reducing peripheral vascular resistance. They do not reduce cardiac output and do not cause reflex tachycardia (selective agents) to a clinically significant degree. They are also metabolically neutral — unlike beta blockers, they do not worsen lipid profiles or mask hypoglycemia, and unlike some antihypertensives, they may actually improve insulin sensitivity slightly.

Despite these pharmacological advantages, the ALLHAT trial finding means alpha blockers are reserved for specific situations rather than used as first-line therapy. Their best current role in hypertension is as adjunctive agents in patients who are not at goal on other drug classes, particularly when BPH is a co-existing condition that makes them doubly beneficial.

When teaching this clinical application, use it as an opportunity to model evidence-based prescribing: mechanistic advantages do not automatically translate into outcome benefits, and clinical trials sometimes overturn what pharmacology predicts. Students who understand this tension are better prepared for evidence-based practice.

### Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)
BPH causes urinary outflow obstruction through two mechanisms: a static component (physical enlargement of the prostate compressing the urethra) and a dynamic component (increased smooth muscle tone in the bladder neck and prostate mediated by α1 receptors). Alpha blockers address the dynamic component — they relax the smooth muscle, reduce urethral resistance, and improve urine flow rate and symptoms of obstruction (hesitancy, weak stream, incomplete emptying, nocturia).

Alpha blockers do not shrink the prostate (5α-reductase inhibitors such as finasteride address the static component) but they work faster — onset of symptom improvement is within days to weeks, compared to months for 5α-reductase inhibitors. For this reason, alpha blockers are often the initial pharmacological choice for symptomatic BPH, with combination therapy added for patients with larger prostates or at higher risk of progression.

The uroselective agents (tamsulosin, alfuzosin) are preferred for BPH in patients without concomitant hypertension because they provide the urological benefit with a more favorable cardiovascular side effect profile. In patients who have both BPH and hypertension, a non-uroselective agent like doxazosin may be preferred to address both conditions simultaneously.

### Pheochromocytoma
Pheochromocytoma is a catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla (or extra-adrenal paraganglia) that causes episodic or sustained hypertension, headache, diaphoresis, and palpitations. The sustained catecholamine excess causes α1-mediated vasoconstriction and hypertension, with the risk of hypertensive crisis during tumor manipulation (including during surgery, physical activity, or even bowel movements).

**Preoperative management:** Phenoxybenzamine is the standard preoperative agent, initiated 1–2 weeks before surgery and titrated until blood pressure is controlled and the patient develops mild orthostatic hypotension (a sign of adequate alpha blockade). Because the blockade is irreversible, it cannot be overcome by intraoperative catecholamine release. Beta blockers are added *after* alpha blockade is established — never before — to control the reflex tachycardia caused by phenoxybenzamine. This sequencing is critical: starting a beta blocker before alpha blockade in a pheochromocytoma patient can cause a paradoxical, potentially fatal hypertensive crisis. With β1 blockade in place but α1 unblocked, the unopposed α1 vasoconstriction from ongoing catecholamine release causes severe hypertension. This is one of the most frequently tested sequencing principles in cardiovascular pharmacology.

**Intraoperative management:** Phentolamine IV is used to control acute hypertensive episodes during surgical manipulation of the tumor. Its short duration and competitive mechanism allow rapid titration, which is ideal in the dynamic intraoperative environment.

**Post-resection hypotension:** After the tumor is removed and catecholamine levels fall precipitously, patients often develop significant hypotension due to the sudden loss of vasopressor tone in a vascular bed that has been chronically vasoconstricted. This is an expected post-operative complication requiring IV fluid resuscitation and vasopressor support if severe. Students should anticipate and recognize this transition.

## Key Adverse Effect — First-Dose Orthostatic Hypotension and Nursing Safety Measures

### The Mechanism
First-dose orthostatic hypotension is the most clinically significant adverse effect of the selective α1 blockers and the reason these drugs require specific administration protocols. Under normal conditions, standing triggers a baroreceptor reflex that causes compensatory α1-mediated vasoconstriction in the lower extremities, maintaining blood pressure during the postural change. Alpha blockers blunt this reflex response — the vasculature cannot constrict adequately to compensate, blood pools in the legs, and blood pressure falls transiently upon standing.

This effect is most pronounced with the **first dose** because the vasculature has no prior exposure to the drug and no compensatory adaptation has occurred. Subsequent doses produce less pronounced hypotension as physiological compensation develops over days to weeks. It is also most pronounced at peak drug concentration, which is why the first dose should be given at a time and place where a fall would not be dangerous.

### Why It Matters
Orthostatic hypotension causes dizziness, lightheadedness, and syncope. In elderly patients — who are a primary population for both BPH and hypertension pharmacotherapy — a syncopal fall can cause hip fracture, head injury, or death. First-dose syncope is not a theoretical risk: it is well-documented in clinical practice and has caused serious patient harm. Students must treat this not as a minor side effect footnote but as a patient safety issue requiring proactive management.

### Nursing Safety Measures

**First-dose administration at bedtime:** The standard protocol for initiating all selective α1 blockers is to give the first dose at bedtime. If orthostatic hypotension or syncope occurs, the patient is already supine and the risk of injury from falling is eliminated. This is a simple, effective, and universally applicable safety measure that must become reflexive for nursing students.

**Dose titration:** Alpha blockers are typically started at the lowest available dose and titrated upward gradually over weeks. Each dose increase carries a re-emergence of first-dose effect risk, though typically milder than the initial dose. Dose escalation should follow the prescriber's titration schedule and not be rushed.

**Patient education — rising slowly:** Patients must be explicitly instructed to change positions slowly: sit on the edge of the bed for one to two minutes before standing, and stand briefly before walking. This gives the cardiovascular system time to compensate. Many patients are not given this instruction clearly and suffer avoidable syncopal episodes.

**Identify high-risk patients:** The orthostatic hypotension risk is amplified in patients who are volume-depleted (from diuretic use, poor oral intake, or hot weather), elderly, on other antihypertensive agents, or have autonomic neuropathy. These patients warrant extra caution, more conservative titration, and closer follow-up after dose initiation or increase.

**Concurrent medications:** Alcohol, other antihypertensives, and PDE5 inhibitors all potentiate the hypotensive effect of alpha blockers. Patients should be counseled to avoid alcohol particularly around the time of dosing and to discuss PDE5 inhibitor use with their prescriber before combining them with an alpha blocker.

**Fall risk assessment:** For hospitalized or long-term care patients initiated on an alpha blocker, complete a formal fall risk assessment and implement appropriate precautions — bed alarm, non-slip footwear, call light within reach, assistance with ambulation for the first several days.

**Monitoring:** Obtain blood pressure in both supine and standing positions (orthostatic vital signs) after initiation and after each dose increase. A drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic or ≥10 mmHg diastolic with standing, particularly if symptomatic, indicates that the current dose may need to be reduced or the titration slowed.
